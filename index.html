Guava is a common tropical fruit cultivated in many tropical and subtropical regions. It is a small tree in the myrtle family, native to Mexico, Central America, the Caribbean, and northern South America1. Guava fruits are commonly eaten raw and are processed into jams, jellies, and preserves2. The tree has mottled greenish bark, long serrated leaves, and white flowers that yield small round, oval, or pear-shaped fruits.

Origin

Origin of guava plant

Guava was adopted as a crop in subtropical and tropical Asia, parts of the United States (from Tennessee and North Carolina, southward, as well as the west and Hawaii), tropical Africa, and Oceania. Guavas were introduced to Florida, US in the 19th century and are grown there as far north as Sarasota, Chipley, Waldo and Fort Pierce. However, they are a primary host of the Caribbean fruit fly and must be protected against infestation in areas of Florida where this pest is present. Guavas are cultivated in several tropical and subtropical countries. Several species are grown commercially; apple guava and its cultivars are those most commonly traded internationally. Medicine: The leaves and fruit of the guava plant have many medicinal uses, including: Digestion: Guava leaf extract can help reduce diarrhea and may benefit people with digestive disorders like irritable bowel syndrome.
